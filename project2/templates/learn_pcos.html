01 — Overview

What is PCOS?

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is one of the most common hormonal disorders affecting women of reproductive age. It affects approximately 1 in 10 women worldwide — yet up to 70% of cases remain undiagnosed.

Despite the name, PCOS doesn't always involve cysts. It's primarily a hormonal imbalance where the ovaries produce excess androgens (male hormones), disrupting the normal menstrual cycle and ovulation.

💡 PCOS is not a single condition — it's a syndrome, meaning it involves a cluster of symptoms that vary from person to person. You don't need all the symptoms to have PCOS.

02 — Signs & Symptoms

Common Symptoms of PCOS

Symptoms vary widely — some women have many, others just a few. The most common include:

🌊

Irregular Periods

Cycles longer than 35 days, fewer than 8 cycles per year, or missing periods for months.

⚖️

Weight Gain

Unexplained weight gain, especially around the abdomen.

😣

Acne & Oily Skin

Hormonal acne that doesn't respond to standard skincare treatments.

💇

Hair Changes

Thinning hair on scalp and unwanted facial/body hair (hirsutism).

😴

Fatigue

Constant exhaustion even after adequate sleep.

🧠

Mood Changes

Anxiety, depression, and mood swings are significantly more common with PCOS.

🤰

Fertility Issues

PCOS is the leading cause of ovulatory infertility.

🌑

Darkened Skin

Dark patches in neck/armpits — a sign of insulin resistance.

03 — Root Causes

What Causes PCOS?

The exact cause of PCOS is not fully understood, but research points to several contributing factors:

  • 🧬
    Genetics: PCOS tends to run in families. If your mother or sister has PCOS, your risk is significantly higher.
  • 💉
    Insulin Resistance: About 70-80% of women with PCOS have insulin resistance.
  • 🔬
    Hormonal Imbalance: Elevated LH and androgens disrupt normal ovulation.
  • 🏭
    Inflammation: Chronic inflammation stimulates androgen production.
  • 🌍
    Environment: Exposure to BPA and pesticides may trigger or worsen PCOS.
04 — Types of PCOS

The Four Types of PCOS

Not all PCOS is the same. Doctors increasingly recognize distinct types based on the root hormonal imbalance:

🔴 Insulin-Resistant PCOS

The most common type (~70%). Best managed with low-glycemic diet and exercise.

💊 Pill-Induced PCOS

Triggered after stopping birth control pills. Usually resolves within 3-6 months.

🔥 Inflammatory PCOS

Chronic inflammation drives androgen production. Anti-inflammatory diet is key.

⚡ Adrenal PCOS

Adrenal glands produce excess DHEA-S in response to stress. Stress management is crucial.

05 — Getting Diagnosed

How is PCOS Diagnosed?

Doctors use the Rotterdam Criteria — you need at least 2 of these 3 features:

🌊

Irregular Periods

Fewer than 8 cycles per year, or cycles longer than 35 days.

🔬

High Androgens

Elevated testosterone/DHEA on blood tests or physical signs like acne/hirsutism.

🩺

Polycystic Ovaries

12 or more follicles in at least one ovary on ultrasound.

Tests your doctor may order:

  • 🩸
    Blood tests: FSH, LH, testosterone, DHEA-S, insulin, glucose, thyroid (TSH), prolactin
  • 🖥
    Pelvic ultrasound to check for ovarian cysts/follicles
  • 📊
    Glucose tolerance test or HbA1c to assess insulin resistance

06 — Treatment Options

PCOS Treatment — What Works?

PCOS has no cure, but its symptoms are very manageable.

💊 Medical Treatments

  • 1
    Metformin: Improves insulin sensitivity, helps regulate cycles
  • 2
    Oral Contraceptives: Regulate cycles and reduce androgens
  • 3
    Anti-androgens (Spironolactone): Reduce acne and hirsutism
  • 4
    Clomid / Letrozole: Stimulate ovulation for fertility
  • 5
    Inositol: Natural supplement improving insulin and egg quality

🌿 Natural Approaches

  • ✓
    Low-glycemic diet (reduces insulin spikes)
  • ✓
    Regular moderate exercise (30 min/day)
  • ✓
    Stress management (yoga, meditation)
  • ✓
    7-9 hours quality sleep per night
  • ✓
    Spearmint tea (reduces androgens)
  • ✓
    Vitamin D, Omega-3, Zinc supplementation
07 — Diet & Lifestyle

Best Diet & Lifestyle for PCOS

Diet is one of the most powerful tools to manage PCOS. Focus on foods that keep blood sugar stable.

✅ Eat More Of...

  • 🥗
    Leafy greens (spinach, kale, methi)
  • 🫘
    Legumes and lentils (dal, rajma, chana)
  • 🐟
    Fatty fish — Omega-3
  • 🥚
    Eggs and lean proteins
  • 🫐
    Berries, apples, pears
  • 🌾
    Millets, oats, brown rice, whole grains
  • 🥑
    Healthy fats: nuts, seeds, avocado

❌ Limit or Avoid...

  • 🚫
    Refined sugar (sweets, cold drinks, juices)
  • 🚫
    White bread, white rice, maida products
  • 🚫
    Processed and packaged foods
  • 🚫
    Fast food and fried items
  • 🚫
    Excess dairy
  • 🚫
    Alcohol and caffeine in excess

💪 Exercise tip: Cardio + strength training 4-5 days/week is ideal. Even a 30-min post-meal walk lowers insulin spikes.

08 — Myths vs Facts

PCOS Myths Debunked

There's a lot of misinformation about PCOS. Here's the truth:

❌ MYTH: "You can't get pregnant with PCOS."
✅ FACT: Many women with PCOS conceive naturally or with medical support.
❌ MYTH: "PCOS only affects overweight women."
✅ FACT: 20-30% of women with PCOS are lean. It affects all body types.
❌ MYTH: "Regular periods mean no PCOS."
✅ FACT: Some PCOS women have regular cycles but still have elevated androgens or polycystic ovaries.
❌ MYTH: "PCOS goes away after pregnancy."
✅ FACT: PCOS is lifelong. Pregnancy may temporarily improve symptoms, but the condition remains.
❌ MYTH: "The birth control pill cures PCOS."
✅ FACT: The pill manages symptoms but doesn't cure PCOS.
